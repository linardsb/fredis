# UK Patents Act 1977 — s.39 (Employee Inventions)

## Statute (paraphrased)

**s.39(1)** — An invention made by an employee belongs to the employer if:

(a) it was made in the course of the employee's **normal duties** or in the course of **duties specifically assigned** to him, and the circumstances were such that an invention **might reasonably be expected to result**; **or**

(b) it was made in the course of the employee's duties **and** at the time the employee had, by reason of the nature of his duties and the particular responsibilities arising from them, a **special obligation to further the interests of the employer's undertaking**.

**s.39(2)** — Any other invention made by an employee belongs to the employee.

Source: legislation.gov.uk, Patents Act 1977, s.39.

## Plain-English rule

An invention (a patentable or potentially-patentable technical advance) belongs to the employer if it falls into either branch of s.39(1). Otherwise it belongs to the employee — this includes:

- inventions made on personal time, outside the scope of assigned duties
- inventions unrelated to the employer's business domain
- inventions made while on gardening leave, sabbatical, or outside employment

## Key factors courts weigh

- What are the employee's **normal duties**? Written contract + actual practice. A developer's normal duties rarely cover inventions outside their delivery scope.
- Was an invention **reasonably expected** to result? Routine coding work usually isn't — R&D roles often are.
- Is there a **special obligation** (seniority, fiduciary duty, director status)? Senior roles attract s.39(1)(b).

## Contract override — the s.42 cap

**s.42** makes any clause in an employee-contract that **diminishes** the employee's rights under s.39 **unenforceable**. An employer cannot contract around s.39(2) to claim ownership of everything an employee invents regardless of context. Broader-than-statute clauses must still respect this floor.

## Practical implication for Fredis + Email Hub

Even if Merkle's contract claims broad IP ownership, s.42 preserves Linards's right to inventions outside his normal duties (e.g. an agentic-AI automation layer, if not part of his role). Each candidate invention must still pass the s.39(1) test individually.
