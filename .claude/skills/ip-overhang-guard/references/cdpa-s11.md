# UK Copyright Designs and Patents Act 1988 — s.11(2)

## Statute (paraphrased)

> Where a literary, dramatic, musical or artistic work, or a film, is made by an employee in the course of his employment, his employer is the first owner of any copyright in the work subject to any agreement to the contrary.

Source: legislation.gov.uk, Copyright Designs and Patents Act 1988, s.11(2).

## Plain-English rule

If an employee writes code, documentation, slide decks, diagrams, or design assets **in the course of employment**, the employer owns the copyright by default. The employee owns it only if:

- the contract expressly reassigns ownership to the employee, or
- the work was made **outside** the course of employment (personal time, personal equipment, unrelated to the employer's business domain).

## Applied tests

Courts consider, among other factors:
- whether the work was done during contracted working hours
- whether the employer's tools, premises, or confidential information were used
- whether the work falls within duties the employee was hired to perform
- whether the employee was paid specifically to produce this kind of work

## Contract override

Most UK employment contracts include an IP clause that goes **broader** than the statutory default — typically asserting ownership over work produced at any time, using any resources, if it relates to the employer's business. Such clauses are usually enforceable but are interpreted against the employer where ambiguous. Overly broad clauses (e.g. claiming all IP the employee ever creates) may be partially unenforceable, but don't rely on this without solicitor advice.
