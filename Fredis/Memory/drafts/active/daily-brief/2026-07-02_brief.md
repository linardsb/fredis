## 2. From your channels

**[IndyDevDan — GLM-5.2 vs MiniMax-M3: Opus Has REAL COMPETITION (Model Stacking)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=cFYdiynrxpQ)** (29 Jun)
Dan argues open-weight models are, for the first time, credibly competitive: GLM-5.2 leads the open field on raw performance, with MiniMax-M3 just behind. His headline is blunt — GLM-5.2 is the better model, MiniMax-M3 is the better deal — so the decision reduces to "maximum capability or optimise for price." He de-hypes the claim that GLM replaces Opus 4.8: they compete on performance and cost, but Opus still leads. His real framework is a three-tier stack — state-of-the-art, workhorse, lightweight — with a max control model (Opus) and a min control model (Qwen 3.6 on local hardware) to make comparisons meaningful. He flags that GLM-5.2 burns a lot of tokens on reasoning, which erodes its speed advantage. The point is owning multiple options rather than betting on one model. **So what:** this is the model-stacking blueprint for Email Hub — tier your calls so the Frontier pillar pays Opus rates only where they earn out.

**[Nate B Jones — The fastest way to make an AI agent dangerous](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CCOoGvX9bCA)** (2 Jul)
Short, sharp thesis: the danger isn't capability, it's diffuse ownership. When an agent can read files, draft messages, change code, summarise customers or update records, someone must own it — operationally, not on an org chart. Letting everyone use it and no one own it is the fast path to harm. It reframes agent risk as an accountability question rather than a model-safety one. **So what:** assign a single operational owner to each Cab and Email Hub agent before you widen access to lead-gen workflows.

**[Unsupervised Learning — Rethinking AI Harnesses](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-YU9Go2vI7k)** (29 Jun)
The pitch: stop thinking of a harness as a coding-assistant toolset and start treating it as an intent-management system. The real bottleneck sits before prompt engineering — translating a fuzzy brain-feeling into text an AI can act on. He's building "Life OS" (formerly Pi) around a harness that holds your current state, desired state, goals and preferences so you can say the smallest possible thing and transfer volumes of context — the "twin sibling glances and just knows" model. Core primitives are current-state-to-ideal-state transitions and a single unified "ideal state artifact" that generalises specs, PRDs and plans. The goal is maximum intent transfer with minimum articulation. It's abstract but points at compression of context as the real product. **So what:** VTV and the Cab are exactly this kind of harness — the moat is the accumulated context that lets a lead-gen agent act on a one-line instruction, not the model behind it.
