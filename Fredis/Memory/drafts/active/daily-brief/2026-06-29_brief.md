## 2. From your channels

[**Frontier results, on device**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fWXJM-J0ZB8) — AI Engineer (29 Jun). Rachel Lee Nabors (Arize) argues that defaulting to frontier models for every task quietly costs money, latency, privacy and uptime. She breaks down cloud inference's hidden costs: data exposure, response delays past the ~4-second believability threshold, uncontrollable third-party spend that compounds in agentic loops, and total failure offline. Her alternative is task-specific and small language models — MobileNet/YOLO for vision, Whisper for audio, Gemma/Qwen for chat — sized in millions to low billions of parameters versus an LLM's billions to trillions. With 8-bit/4-bit quantisation a 1B model fits in ~2GB and runs on-device; her Pixel 10 Pro already ships with one. She cites NVIDIA's 2025 paper calling SLMs the future of agentic AI and matching or beating large models on energy for narrow jobs, demoed via Arize's open-source Phoenix. **So what:** Email Hub / Cab — route narrow jobs (classification, summarisation, "is this rude?") to on-device SLMs to cut inference cost and latency and keep user data local.

[**We're frozen out (for good?)**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=61dz7Fh0ozg) — David Shapiro (26 Jun). Reacts to the government holding GPT-5.6 and strong-arming Anthropic into pulling Mythos and Fable, set against Aschenbrenner's thesis that frontier access would be restricted around 2026–27. His call is not a blip or a slowdown but a bifurcation: flagship models reserved for cleared and top-paying corporate use, watered-down general models for everyone else. He concedes it is dystopian but argues technology has always stratified. On cyber he is contrarian: broad access to Mythos-level models would be net-positive because AI automates best practice, penetration testing, auditing and log analysis read-only. He frames humans as the residual weakest link regardless. **So what:** Frontier pillar — assume your best model tier becomes gated; design Email Hub and Cab to degrade gracefully onto the general tier.

[**India Can Create The Largest AI Companies**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Ju8LVdvuxGM) — YC Root Access (27 Jun). Ex-YC partners Puneet (SuperDaily, ~$100M ARR on effectively one engineer, exited to Swiggy) and Arnav (Peak XV) close out a YC India session. Core claim: for the first time founders can build global companies from India because the AI wave, unlike mobile, is not local. Mobile tokenised labour and produced hyper-local network effects; AI is global, and everyone worldwide grasps its importance at once, removing the old need to relocate to SF. Puneet's framing: this wave rewards living at the edge of the technology and understanding it 10x better than anyone, more than GTM or business-model polish. They cite Giga and Emergent selling globally without ever living in SF. **So what:** lead generation — your buyer is now a lean, technically-deep global founder; depth of AI capability beats geography.

[**Is the junior developer pipeline collapsing?**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=f1Ulp-ZMWy8) — Nate B Jones (29 Jun). Cites a 2025 Harvard study finding junior-developer employment drops 9% within six quarters of widespread AI coding-tool adoption. Jones argues the figure understates it — people early in their careers say the squeeze is worse than the data shows. His blunt thesis: the junior pipeline is starting to collapse as routine entry-level work is automated. The transcript is heavily truncated (~50 words), so mitigations and supporting argument are not captured. **So what:** lead generation / Cab — if entry-level engineering hiring contracts, tooling that lets tiny teams ship is precisely the pain to sell against.
