## 2. From your channels

[GLM-5.2 vs MiniMax-M3: Opus Has REAL COMPETITION (IndyDevDan)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=cFYdiynrxpQ) — Argues open-weight models are, for the first time, real competition to Opus, with GLM 5.2 leading the open field and MiniMax M3 just behind. He frames a three-tier stack — state-of-the-art, workhorse, lightweight — rather than picking one model. The trade-off: GLM 5.2 is the better model, MiniMax M3 the better deal, so the decision reduces to maximum capability versus price. He is blunt that GLM cannot replace Opus 4.8 — they compete on performance and cost, but Opus still leads on raw capability. A recurring theme is ownership and resiliency: many providers serve open weights, so a lab or government cannot kill your agents. He flags GLM 5.2's heavy reasoning-token usage as a real cost catch despite its speed. So what: a stacked strategy de-risks the Frontier pillar — Opus where it matters, cheap open weights for VTV/Cab and lead-gen workloads where price and uptime beat the last few points of quality.

[The Real Story Behind the Government GPT 5.6 Freeze (Nate B Jones)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H9oNA5IyrXA) — Reports GPT-5.6 shipped only to a small group of approved partners while a cyber-security review runs — a slowdown, not a cancellation. His core claim: if frontier intelligence stalls even briefly, the advantage is not the newest model but the context that makes any model useful — which message matters, which file is current, what was decided, what can be shared. He ties together Siri reaching into calendar/email/photos/screen, Claude in Slack with scoped data access, and Codex as OpenAI's dominant internal work surface. The shared problem: a smart model still doesn't know what's going on, so users burn effort pasting context in. The winner knows where the work lives, what it may see, and what it may do — seamlessly. Intelligence is getting cheaper while the freshest frontier ships more slowly. So what: this is the exact Email Hub thesis — own the context layer over a user's mail and decisions and you win even when the model is commoditised.

[Building Great Agent Skills: The Missing Manual (AI Engineer)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UNzCG3lw6O0) — A talk on writing great agent skills, framed around escaping "skill hell": piling up skills nobody can tell good from bad. The checklist is trigger (how it's invoked), internal structure, how it steers the agent, then pruning to the smallest working form. Key distinction: user-invoked versus model-invoked skills — model-invocable skills carry a description that sits in the agent's context as a "context pointer," while user-only skills hide it via disable-model-invocation. That description is what lets the agent decide to pull the full skill.md into context itself. The advice is itself encoded as a "writing great skills" skill so you can apply it immediately. The thrust is rubric-driven quality over collecting more skills. So what: a disciplined rubric raises Cab/VTV agent reliability — fewer noop skills, sharper triggers, smaller context footprints, cheaper and more predictable lead-gen automations.

[India Can Create The Largest AI Companies (YC Root Access)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Ju8LVdvuxGM) — Former YC partners argue this is the first wave letting India build genuinely global, not hyper-local, companies. Previous waves (mobile) tokenised local labour and produced regional network-effect businesses; AI is global, so the edge goes to whoever understands the technology 10x better than anyone else. Puneet recounts running SuperDaily to $100m ARR with effectively one engineer — before AI — as a preview of how lean AI-native teams can now be. They claim US distribution no longer needs years in San Francisco or warm intros, because everyone worldwide grasped AI's importance simultaneously. The emphasis shifts from go-to-market mastery to living at the frontier of the technology, citing Emergent and Giga selling globally without an SF base. So what: lean, AI-native, globally-distributed building is the template for VTV/Cab — small teams reaching customers anywhere validates a low-headcount, frontier-first approach to lead generation.

[Rethinking AI Harnesses (Unsupervised Learning)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-YU9Go2vI7k) — Argues we've mis-framed harnesses as coding tools when they're really intent-management systems. The chain runs from a vague feeling, to words, to a captured prompt, to the AI understanding it — and the harness's job is to compress that path. He's building Life OS (formerly Pi), where the harness is your whole life: goals, current state, desired state, preferences and constraints, so coding and life context blur into one. The aim is to say as little as possible yet transfer full intent — like a synced twin who glances at something and knows "does this work for us?". Two built-in concepts: current-state-to-ideal-state transitions, and a single unified "ideal state artifact" that generalises specs, PRDs and prompts. The bet is context compression over more tools or benchmarks. So what: "harness as context store" is adjacent to Email Hub — capture a user's standing intent and state once, then act with minimal prompting, the same moat applied to mail and tasks.

[We're frozen out (for good?) (David Shapiro)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=61dz7Fh0ozg) — Stale (2026-06-26, four days old); covers the same frontier-access debate (GPT-5.6 held back, Mythos/Fable pulled) already captured fresher by Nate B Jones.

[Learn anything with the /teach skill (Matt Pocock)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=s5T5oQJcJ6U) — Stale (2026-06-08, three weeks old); builds a stateful "teach" skill, no new signal for the Frontier pillar.

[Why is Serena Williams really back at Wimbledon? (20VC)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3sl2PJU6mqY) — Off-topic (sport/celebrity GLP-1 marketing); nothing for Email Hub, VTV, Cab or lead generation.
